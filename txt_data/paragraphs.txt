This project illuminates which topics dominate The Guardian’s articles about climate change and attempts to undercover which narratives may be being sidelined in big media. The issue with marginalised climate narratives is one of hermeneutical injustice. As described by Langton (2010), hermeneutical injustice is when citizens face a lack of proper terminology and exposure to realities such that they cannot sufficiently express themselves. The marginalisation of climate narratives, for example the existence of climate feedback loops and the threat of climate tipping points, restrict citizen’s knowledge of climate threats and terminology. Hence political agency becomes limited.
Classical realism posits that state behaviour is explained by a need to maintain power and survive against all other states (Morgenthau, 1948). We can employ classical realism as a superconcept (Gombrich & Peterson, 2024), in other words extract the idea of power dynamics out of its origin in the discipline of international relations and into our question of media narratives. Here classical realism explains that The Guardian and other news organisations must publish popular, relevant, and attention-grabbing articles because of their inherent need for survival, the continuation of the organisation, and the continuation of sharing news articles. Therefore, one must be careful when critiquing the dominance of articles in the news. It remains important to highlight their dominance and to question why other important climate topics are not appearing in news articles. However, this must come alongside a reflection of the news organisation’s ability to do that. Should a news article become hyper specific, e.g. about the impact of climate change on a forest near Bristol it likely won’t engage a national audience, putting The Guardian’s ability to sustain themselves slightly more at risk.
One solution to the complex problem of hermeneutical injustice in climate narratives is awareness and education. Hence the graph below, sharing my findings. There is potential for an extension to this project which scrap the internet for the latest news articles from a wide array of sources – new books, journal articles, news articles from many organisations, etc. – to provide a more complete and climate-focused webpage, enabling anyone to access climate knowledge, while forgoing the requirements popularity demanded by classical realism because of its independent non-profit status.
